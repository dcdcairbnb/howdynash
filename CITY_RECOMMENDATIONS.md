## Selection Criteria

What makes a city right for this model.

Tourism volume. 5 million+ annual visitors needed for meaningful revenue.

Distinct identity. Easy to brand and curate. Generic suburbs do not work.

Strong music or food scene. The bot's personality leans into local culture.

Concentrated tourist district. Walkable downtown helps recommendations land.

Bachelorette/bachelor party tourism. High-LTV segment that books experiences and stays.

Underserved by quality apps. Avoid markets dominated by official tourism apps.

Affiliate revenue density. Hotels, tours, restaurants concentrated in walking distance.

Similar cultural feel to Nashville. Southern hospitality tone transfers naturally.

## Top 5 Candidate Cities

### 1. New Orleans (Best Match)

Annual visitors: 19 million.

Why it works.

Music city counterpart. Jazz to Nashville's country.

Concentrated tourist district (French Quarter). Easy to recommend.

Distinctive cuisine (Cajun, Creole, beignets) that begs curation.

Massive bachelorette/bachelor tourism.

Strong cocktail and bar culture for nightlife recommendations.

Mardi Gras and other festivals year-round.

Visit New Orleans (official) is an event-focused site, not a chatbot.

Why it might not.

More established competitors than Nashville.

Different cultural register (Cajun vs Southern). Tone needs adjustment.

Hurricane risk affects tourism patterns.

Branding angle.

"Howdy NOLA" or "Howdy New Orleans"

Domain: howdynola.com or howdyneworleans.com

Avatar: saxophone or fleur-de-lis

Tone: keep friendly, slightly more sophisticated

Estimated launch effort: 12 to 15 hours.

Estimated revenue potential: equal to or higher than Nashville.

### 2. Austin

Annual visitors: 35 million.

Why it works.

"Live Music Capital of the World." Direct sister city to Nashville.

Tech-savvy visitor base. Comfortable with chatbots.

Massive food scene (BBQ, Tex-Mex, food trucks).

Strong festival economy (SXSW, ACL, Formula 1).

Walking-friendly downtown (6th Street).

Visit Austin app exists but is dated.

Why it might not.

Higher cost of living means hotels are pricier (good for affiliate revenue).

Tech industry competition for hotel marketing dollars.

Visitors are more diverse (corporate vs leisure mix).

Branding angle.

"Howdy Austin" or "Yall Austin" if testing different format

Domain: howdyaustin.com

Avatar: cowboy hat or Texas-shape

Tone: keep friendly Southern voice

Estimated launch effort: 12 to 15 hours.

Estimated revenue potential: HIGHER than Nashville (more visitors, higher spend).

### 3. Charleston, SC

Annual visitors: 7 million.

Why it works.

Foodie destination (named America's #1 food city multiple years).

Distinct identity (historic, southern, coastal).

Concentrated tourist area (King Street, Market).

Bachelorette tourism is huge.

Quality hotel scene (Belmond, The Restoration, Wentworth).

Less competition than NOLA or Austin.

Visit Charleston is mostly a directory site.

Why it might not.

Smaller tourism volume.

Hurricane season affects 25% of year.

Higher-end visitors expect polished apps.

Branding angle.

"Howdy Charleston"

Domain: howdycharleston.com

Avatar: pineapple (Charleston symbol of hospitality)

Tone: more refined, still warm

Estimated launch effort: 10 to 12 hours.

Estimated revenue potential: EQUAL to Nashville with higher per-conversion value.

### 4. Memphis

Annual visitors: 12 million.

Why it works.

Sister city to Nashville. Country/blues music. BBQ. Soul food.

Beale Street equivalent of Broadway.

Graceland, civil rights history, Sun Studio.

Underserved by quality tourism apps.

Tone transfers directly from Nashville build.

Could share infrastructure heavily.

Why it might not.

Smaller tourist economy than Nashville.

Lower-spend visitor demographics.

Crime perception affects bookings.

Branding angle.

"Howdy Memphis"

Domain: howdymemphis.com

Avatar: guitar or BBQ pig

Tone: identical to Nashville

Estimated launch effort: 8 to 10 hours (shortest, most code reuse).

Estimated revenue potential: 60% of Nashville.

### 5. Savannah, GA

Annual visitors: 16 million.

Why it works.

Picturesque historic district. Iconic.

Food and ghost tour heavy. High activity bookings.

Walkable downtown.

Bachelorette tourism strong.

Quaint hotel scene (boutique-heavy).

Tone matches Nashville hospitality vibe.

Why it might not.

Sleepier nightlife than Nashville.

Smaller market.

Visit Savannah app is decent.

Branding angle.

"Howdy Savannah"

Domain: howdysavannah.com

Avatar: Spanish moss or oak tree

Tone: gentler, more historic-leaning

Estimated launch effort: 10 to 12 hours.

Estimated revenue potential: 70% of Nashville.

## My Top Pick

### Choose New Orleans for City 2

Reasoning.

Highest absolute revenue potential of any candidate. 19 million visitors with high spend.

Strongest narrative parallel. "Two music cities of the South: Nashville for country, NOLA for jazz." That story sells.

Most distinct culture. Forces you to truly localize the curated data, validating the playbook.

If the model works for both Nashville and NOLA, it will work for any tourism market.

Best bachelorette/bachelor crossover. Many of those parties hit both cities.

Easiest to brand. NOLA is iconic. The Howdy NOLA tagline writes itself.

Affiliate density is excellent. Tours, hotels, restaurants all concentrated.

Why not the safer picks first.

Memphis is too similar to Nashville. Building it would not test the playbook on something different.

Charleston is good but smaller. Lower revenue ceiling.

Austin is great but more competitive.

Savannah is good but sleepy.

NOLA forces you to handle a culturally distinct city while still leveraging the Southern hospitality angle. It is the highest signal test of whether the model scales.

### Sequence I would follow

City 1: Nashville (current).

City 2: New Orleans (test cultural variance).

City 3: Charleston or Austin (depending on which feels easier given resources).

City 4 to 5: Memphis and Savannah (easy adds for the Howdy brand portfolio).

After 5 cities. Decide whether to expand within the South (Atlanta, Louisville, Asheville) or jump regions (Las Vegas, Miami).

## Cities to AVOID

New York City. Too saturated. Too expensive to acquire users. Visit NYC and a dozen other apps already cover it.

Los Angeles. Same. Sprawling, hard to curate, expensive.

Chicago. Generic in tourism positioning. No clear hook.

Las Vegas. High potential but extremely competitive. Casinos own affiliate dollars.

San Francisco. Tourism declining. Tech apps already cover it.

Generic suburbs or small towns. Not enough density.

International cities. Different regulatory regime, different affiliate networks, different language.

## Cities Worth Watching

Asheville, NC. Booming tourism, beer/foodie scene, distinct mountain identity.

Louisville, KY. Bourbon trail, Kentucky Derby, sister to Nashville.

Boulder, CO. Outdoor + foodie + tech crowd.

Greenville, SC. Underrated, growing fast.

Atlanta, GA. Bigger market but genericism risk.

Miami, FL. High potential but competitive.

Branson, MO. Underserved, niche family/country tourism.

Oxford, MS. Tiny but growing as a foodie destination.

## Decision Framework

Use this to evaluate any city in the future.

Score each criterion 1 to 5.

Annual visitors over 10M (5 = yes, 1 = under 1M).

Distinct identity (5 = iconic, 1 = generic).

Music/food culture (5 = nationally known, 1 = none).

Walkable downtown (5 = compact, 1 = sprawling).

Bachelorette/bachelor draw (5 = top 5 destination, 1 = none).

Underserved by apps (5 = no quality apps, 1 = saturated).

Hotel density (5 = 100+ in tourist district, 1 = under 20).

Cultural fit with Howdy brand (5 = perfect match, 1 = wrong region).

Score totals.

35 to 40: Top priority. Launch immediately if resources allow.

28 to 34: Good fit. Solid second-tier candidate.

20 to 27: Possible but risky.

Under 20: Skip.
